What to Avoid — Often Recommended, Evidence Doesn't Support It for You

High-Dose Vitamin C / Vitamin E

This is counterintuitive but the research is clear: high-dose antioxidants (>500 mg Vit C or >400 IU Vit E daily) blunt training adaptations. Exercise works by creating controlled oxidative stress — your body responds by building mitochondria, increasing VO2max, improving fat oxidation. Flooding your system with antioxidants suppresses this signaling. Multiple RCTs confirm reduced training adaptation with chronic high-dose supplementation. Small amounts from food (fruits, vegetables) are completely fine and beneficial.

Rhodiola Rosea for Altitude

Widely marketed for altitude adaptation. The evidence is more negative than positive — several controlled studies found no benefit for altitude performance or AMS symptoms. The studies showing benefit are mostly low-quality. Not worth the cost or the unknown interactions. Skip it.

Acetazolamide (Diamox) without a Doctor

Prescription diuretic that forces acclimatization by acidifying blood. Works for preventing AMS but causes significant side effects (tingling, frequent urination, taste disturbance, carbonate drink aversion — which includes sports drinks). At 8,700 ft you're unlikely to get true AMS. Talk to your doctor, but for most runners at SMU altitude it's overkill and the side effects hurt performance.

Creatine during Peak Training

Good supplement for many contexts (strength, power, cognitive function), but during peak ultra training it causes ~1–3 kg of water weight retention, which is dead weight over 54 miles. Fine to use in off-season or after the race. Stop 8–10 weeks before a goal race if you currently take it.

Iron without Testing

Taking iron supplements when you're not deficient does nothing for performance and can cause GI distress, constipation, and over time actual toxicity. The potential harm from unnecessary iron supplementation is real. Test, don't guess.

Most "Altitude" Branded Products

Acli-Mate, OxyShot, and similar products have minimal controlled trial evidence. They typically contain ginkgo biloba (weak evidence), antioxidants (potentially counterproductive during training), and herbs with no meaningful RCT support. The markup is high, the evidence is thin.
